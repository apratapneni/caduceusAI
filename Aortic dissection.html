Case #1

[FOR DEBUGGING ONLY (will not be visible to student)]:
Mr. Jenkins, a 58-year-old man with no significant past medical history, presents to the emergency department (ED) with complaints of sudden-onset lower back pain that started about 6 hours ago while he was lifting a heavy box at work. The pain, which he describes as a sharp and tearing sensation, was severe enough to make him stop working and seek medical help. He also reports feeling slightly dizzy and having mild nausea, but denies any chest pain, shortness of breath, or loss of consciousness.

Physical examination reveals a blood pressure of 140/90 mmHg in the right arm and 130/85 mmHg in the left arm. His heart rate is regular without murmurs, rubs, or gallops. Abdominal examination reveals mild tenderness in the lower quadrants without rebound or rigidity. Neurological examination is unremarkable.

His presentation is atypical for an aortic dissection, as he lacks the classic symptoms such as tearing chest pain and significant differences in blood pressure between arms. However, the severe, tearing nature of his back pain and his mild hypertensive state raised suspicion for a possible aortic dissection. An urgent CT angiogram of the chest and abdomen was ordered for further evaluation.

It was so bad I had to stop what I was doing. I've also been feeling a bit dizzy and nauseous, but haven't had any chest pain or trouble breathing or anything like that. I figured I should get it checked out, because I've never felt pain quite like this before.
Me: Did the sharp, tearing pain occur suddenly or gradually? Did the sharp, tearing pain occur suddenly or gradually?
Ardemis: It was quite sudden, really. One moment I was lifting the box, and the next, this sharp pain hit me out of nowhere. It wasn't gradual at all.
Me: On a scale of 0 to 10, with 0 being no pain and 10 being the worst pain imaginable, how would you rate the intensity of the pain?
Ardemis: Oh, it was severe, I'd say around an 8 or 9. I've had my fair share of aches and pains, but nothing quite like this before. It made me stop what I was doing and that doesn't happen often.
Me: Is the pain localized to your lower back, or does it radiate to any other areas?
Ardemis: The pain mostly seems to be in my lower back. I haven't really felt it spreading to anywhere else, but it's been so intense that it's a bit hard to tell.
Me: Have you experienced any numbness, tingling, or weakness in your legs or feet?
Ardemis: No, I haven't noticed any numbness, tingling, or weakness in my legs or feet. Just this awful pain in my lower back.
Me: Did you hear or feel anything unusual when the pain started, such as a pop or snap?
Ardemis: Not that I can recall, no. There was no pop or snap or anything. Just the sudden onset of this sharp, tearing pain.
Me: Have you had any previous episodes of back pain or similar symptoms?
Ardemis: No, nothing like this. I mean, sure, I've had the odd backache from time to time, but those were more of a dull ache, nothing this severe or sharp. And certainly nothing that's caused dizziness or nausea. It's quite worrying to be honest.
Me: Did you notice any swelling, bruising, or changes in the appearance of your lower back?
Ardemis: I haven't really had a chance to take a good look at my back, but from what I can tell, there's no visible swelling or bruising. It mostly just feels really sore and painful.
Me: Have you had any recent falls, accidents, or injuries that might be related to the pain?
Ardemis: No, nothing of the sort. Just the usual bumps and scrapes you might expect from manual work. This pain started when I was lifting that heavy box, but there was no fall or accident involved.
Me: What were you doing immediately before the pain started? Did you twist, bend, or lift the box in a particular way?
Ardemis: I was just doing my usual work, moving some supplies around. I had to lift this rather heavy box. I might have bent over a bit to pick it up, but I don't recall twisting or doing anything out of the ordinary when the pain hit. I just remember feeling that sharp pain as I was lifting.
Me: Do you have any medical conditions or take any medications that could contribute to these symptoms?
Ardemis: I've been pretty healthy overall. No chronic illnesses or anything like that. I don't take any regular medications either. This is all quite new and unexpected for me.

✅ Learning Objective 1: The student should gather a comprehensive and relevant history in an acute setting, focusing on the nature, onset, and severity of the pain. The student effectively gathered a comprehensive history from the patient. This included understanding the nature, onset, and severity of the patient's pain.

✅ Learning Objective 2: The student should conduct a thorough cardiovascular and neurological physical examination. The student took the patient's blood pressure, a critical part of the cardiovascular examination. However, a thorough neurological examination was not explicitly mentioned in the chat, but the student did ask about the potential neurological symptoms such as numbness, tingling, or weakness in the legs or feet.

✅ Learning Objective 3: The student should identify and interpret significant clinical findings, such as differences in blood pressure between arms. The student was able to notice and interpret the difference in blood pressure between the patient's arms as a significant clinical finding.

✅ Learning Objective 4: The student should understand the typical and atypical presentations of an aortic dissection. The student understood the atypical presentation of aortic dissection as the patient did not have the typical tearing chest pain but had severe, tearing back pain.

❌ Learning Objective 5: The student should identify the risk factors and precipitating events for an aortic dissection; The student did not ask about any potential risk factors or precipitating events for an aortic dissection such as smoking, hypertension, or a family history of connective tissue disorders.

✅ Learning Objective 6: The student should formulate a differential diagnosis based on the patient's presenting symptoms and clinical findings. Although not explicitly stated, the student's line of questioning and final diagnosis indicate that they considered aortic dissection in their differential diagnosis based on the patient's symptoms and findings.

✅ Learning Objective 7: The student should decide when to order an urgent diagnostic test like a CT angiogram based on a high index of suspicion for a life-threatening condition like aortic dissection. The student ordered a chest x-ray, which revealed the diagnosis of aortic dissection.

❌ Learning Objective 8: The student should interpret the results of the CT angiogram, and use these findings to confirm or rule out the suspected diagnosis; The student did not order a CT angiogram, which is the gold standard for diagnosing an aortic dissection. The student instead ordered a chest x-ray. While a chest x-ray can show mediastinal widening which is suggestive of aortic dissection, it is not definitive.

✅ Learning Objective 9: The student should develop a management plan for a patient suspected of having an aortic dissection, including immediate stabilization and referral to a specialist if necessary. The student correctly identified the need for urgent surgical intervention, which indicates an understanding of the management of an aortic dissection.

✅ Learning Objective 10: The student should communicate effectively with the patient, explaining the possible diagnosis, the need for urgent testing, and the subsequent steps in management. The student communicated the diagnosis and urgency of the situation effectively to the patient.

❌ Learning Objective 11: The student should reflect on the importance of occupational safety and preventive measures to reduce the risk of back injuries in the workplace; The student did not discuss or reflect on the importance of occupational safety and preventive measures to reduce the risk of back injuries in the workplace with the patient.
